# Origin Guard - Planning Document

## Architecture

### Submission Flow
1. Creator sends text to POST /submit
2. Rate limiter checks request volume
3. Signal 1 (Groq LLM) scores the text
4. Signal 2 (Stylometric Heuristics) scores the text
5. Both scores combine into one confidence score
6. Confidence score maps to a transparency label
7. Everything gets written to the audit log
8. Response returns to the creator with result and label

### Appeal Flow
1. Creator sends POST /appeal with content_id and reasoning
2. Status updates to "under_review"
3. Appeal gets logged in the audit log
4. Creator gets confirmation back

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1 - LLM Classifier
- **What it measures:** Semantic patterns, writing style,
and holistic feel of the text
- **Output:** A score between 0 and 1 where 1 means
very likely AI and 0 means very likely human
- **Blind spot:** Can misclassify formal or precise human
writers as AI, particularly non-native English speakers
and academics

### Signal 2 - Stylometric Heuristics
- **What it measures:** Statistical properties of the text
including sentence length variance, type-token ratio
(vocabulary diversity), informal punctuation density, and
average sentence length
- **Output:** A score between 0 and 1 where 1 means
very likely AI and 0 means very likely human
- **Blind spot:** Cannot read meaning at all, so heavily
edited AI text with added irregularities may fool it.
Additionally, the type-token ratio metric specifically is
unreliable on short passages (under ~50 words), since any
coherent short text — human or AI — tends to have high
vocabulary diversity simply due to length, not origin. This
was confirmed during Milestone 4 testing and is documented
further in the README's Known Limitations section.

### Combining Signals
Final confidence score is a weighted average:
```
final_score = (llm_score * 0.55) + (stylo_score * 0.45)
```

This was originally 0.6/0.4 in favor of the LLM signal. After
testing with real examples in Milestone 4, the stylometric
signal's weight was increased slightly to 0.45 once its TTR
weakness was identified and partially compensated for by the
sentence-length and punctuation metrics carrying more relative
influence. The LLM still carries marginally more weight overall
because it captures meaning and context, which is harder to
fake than statistical structure alone.

## Uncertainty Representation

| Score Range | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| Above 0.75 | High confidence AI |
| 0.45 - 0.75 | Uncertain |
| Below 0.45 | High confidence human |

A score of 0.51 and 0.95 produce meaningfully different
labels. 0.51 falls in the uncertain range while 0.95
triggers the high confidence AI label.

## Transparency Label

### High Confidence AI (score > 0.75)
"This content appears to be AI-generated. If you believe
this is incorrect, you may submit an appeal."

### Uncertain (score between 0.45 and 0.75)
"This content shows mixed signals. We were unable to
determine with confidence whether it was written by a
human or generated by AI. If you believe this is
incorrect, you may submit an appeal."

### High Confidence Human (score < 0.45)
"This content appears to be written by a human."

## Appeals Workflow

### What creators provide:
- content_id: the ID of the submission they are appealing
- creator_reasoning: their explanation for why the
classification is wrong

### What happens when an appeal is filed:
- Submission status updates from "classified"
to "under_review"
- Appeal is logged alongside the original decision

### What gets logged:
- Original content ID
- Original classification and confidence score
- Creator reasoning
- Timestamp
- New status "under_review"

## Anticipated Edge Cases

### Edge Case 1 - Formal Human Writers
A non-native English speaker or academic writer who writes
very formally and precisely. Their clean structured writing
style statistically resembles AI output so both signals
may wrongly flag them as AI. This is why the appeals
workflow is critical.

### Edge Case 2 - Heavily Edited AI Text
A user takes AI generated text and heavily edits it by
adding irregular sentence lengths, varied vocabulary and
typos. The stylometric heuristics may score it as human
because the structure looks messy while the LLM may also
be fooled because the meaning and flow has been altered
enough to seem natural. The system would likely return
an uncertain or wrongly human classification.

### Edge Case 3 - Short-Form AI Content
A short piece of AI-generated text (a caption, a single
paragraph excerpt) may score lower on the stylometric signal
than a longer AI sample would, because the type-token ratio
metric loses discriminating power below roughly 50 words.
This was discovered during Milestone 4 testing rather than
anticipated up front, and is now documented as a known
limitation rather than fully solved.

## AI Tool Plan

### Milestone 3 - Submission endpoint and Signal 1
- Spec sections to provide: Detection Signals + Architecture
- Ask AI to generate: Flask app skeleton with POST /submit
route and the Signal 1 Groq function
- Verify by: testing the function independently with a few
inputs before wiring into the endpoint

### Milestone 4 - Signal 2 and confidence scoring
- Spec sections to provide: Detection Signals +
Uncertainty Representation + Architecture
- Ask AI to generate: Signal 2 stylometric function
and scoring logic that combines both signals
- Verify by: testing with clearly AI and clearly human
text to confirm scores differ meaningfully. This testing is
what surfaced the TTR weakness on short passages, and led to
the weight rebalancing described above.

### Milestone 5 - Production layer
- Spec sections to provide: Transparency Label +
Appeals Workflow + Architecture
- Ask AI to generate: label generation function
and POST /appeal endpoint
- Verify by: confirming all three label variants are
reachable and that an appeal updates status correctly
